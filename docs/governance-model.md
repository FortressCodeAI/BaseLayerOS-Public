# BaseLayerOS Governance Model

The BaseLayerOS Governance Model defines **how rules, identities, workflows, data, and evolution** are controlled across the substrate.  
It is the core of what makes BaseLayerOS a **governance‑native operating system**, not just an agent framework.

This document explains:

- how governance flows through the 14‑layer stack
- how constitutions become executable rules
- how identity binds to execution
- how workflows are governed
- how data access is controlled
- how decisions are explained
- how evolution is safely managed

---

# 1. Purpose of the Governance Model

The governance model ensures that:

- **nothing runs without identity**
- **nothing executes without policy evaluation**
- **nothing violates regulatory constraints**
- **nothing escapes forensic capture**
- **nothing evolves without simulation and approval**

Governance is not a bolt‑on.  
It is the **substrate**.

---

# 2. Governance Flow Overview

Governance flows through BaseLayerOS in a structured pipeline:

1. **Constitutions**
2. **Policies & regulatory obligations**
3. **Executable predicates**
4. **Identity binding**
5. **Workflow constraints**
6. **Data access rules**
7. **Runtime enforcement**
8. **Forensic capture**
9. **Explainability**
10. **Governed evolution**

Each stage is enforced by specific layers of the substrate.

---

# 3. Constitutions

A **constitution** is a high‑level governance document that defines:

- organizational principles
- regulatory obligations
- safety boundaries
- ethical constraints
- operational rules

Constitutions are:

- human‑readable
- versioned
- auditable
- traceable

Examples:

- “All actions must be identity‑bound.”
- “High‑risk decisions require human approval.”
- “Personal health information must remain within jurisdiction X.”
- “Agents may not self‑modify or escalate authority.”

Constitutions are ingested by the **Constitutional Enforcement Layer (CEL)**.

---

# 4. Policies & Regulatory Obligations

Policies include:

- internal governance rules
- sector‑specific requirements
- regulatory obligations
- contractual constraints
- risk thresholds

These are interpreted by the **Regulatory Interpretation Engine (RIE)**.

RIE maps:

- PHIPA → data access predicates
- OSFI B‑13 → identity & audit predicates
- GDPR → purpose limitation predicates
- EU AI Act → high‑risk system predicates
- NIST AI RMF → risk evaluation predicates

This creates a **policy graph** that CEL and WCF enforce.

---

# 5. Executable Predicates

Policies and constitutions are compiled into **executable predicates**.

A predicate is:

- machine‑enforceable
- deterministic
- explainable
- versioned
- auditable

Predicates are applied at:

- action time
- workflow transitions
- data access requests
- agent communication
- evolution proposals

Predicates are enforced by:

- CEL
- RIE
- WCF
- GDAL
- NAS

---

# 6. Identity Binding

Identity is the foundation of governance.

The **Identity‑Bound Execution Layer (IBEL)** ensures:

- every action has a principal
- every workflow step has an actor
- every agent runs under a bound identity
- permissions are explicit and non‑escalating

Identity flows through:

1. Enterprise IAM → Identity Adapter
2. Identity Adapter → IBEL
3. IBEL → DMAR
4. DMAR → ITFS (forensic identity capture)

Identity is harmonized across industries by **CIIPL**.

---

# 7. Workflow Governance

Workflows are governed by the **Workflow Compliance Fabric (WCF)**.

WCF enforces:

- approvals
- segregation of duties
- risk thresholds
- regulatory constraints
- constitutional rules
- workflow‑specific policies

WCF integrates with:

- ServiceNow
- Jira
- SAP
- Salesforce
- EHR systems
- banking workflow engines
- government workflow systems

via the **Workflow & Event Adapter (WEA)**.

---

# 8. Data Governance

Data access is governed by the **Governed Data Access Layer (GDAL)**.

GDAL enforces:

- purpose limitation
- minimum necessary access
- jurisdictional constraints
- masking & redaction
- PHI/PII handling rules
- financial data controls
- cross‑border restrictions

GDAL evaluates:

- identity
- purpose
- workflow context
- regulatory predicates
- constitutional constraints

All decisions are logged in ITFS.

---

# 9. Runtime Governance

The **Deterministic Multi‑Agent Runtime (DMAR)** enforces governance during execution.

DMAR ensures:

- deterministic behavior
- no bypass of governance layers
- no self‑modification
- no unauthorized communication
- no nondeterministic side effects

DMAR integrates with:

- CEL
- RIE
- WCF
- GDAL
- NAS

to enforce governance at runtime.

---

# 10. Forensic Governance

The **Immutable Temporal Forensics Substrate (ITFS)** provides:

- tamper‑evident logs
- identity‑bound evidence
- replayability
- regulatory‑grade auditability

ITFS ensures:

- every action is recorded
- every decision is explainable
- every workflow is reconstructable
- every evolution is traceable

ITFS integrates with SIEMs via the **Forensics & Logging Adapter (FLA)**.

---

# 11. Explainability Governance

The **Explainable Decisioning Engine (EDE)** provides:

- human‑readable explanations
- predicate‑level reasoning
- workflow context
- regulatory justification
- identity attribution

EDE ensures:

- decisions are not opaque
- explanations are grounded in actual predicates
- regulators can understand system behavior

---

# 12. Evolution Governance

The **Autonomous Evolution Pipeline (AEP)** governs how BaseLayerOS evolves.

AEP enforces:

- simulation
- risk evaluation
- certainty thresholds
- human approval
- rollback
- forensic recording

AEP ensures:

- no silent changes
- no untested updates
- no unbounded evolution
- no bypass of governance

---

# 13. Hard Safety Governance

The **No‑Abuse Substrate (NAS)** provides a **non‑bypassable safety floor**.

NAS blocks:

- destructive actions
- harmful operations
- prohibited behaviors
- unsafe agent patterns

NAS enforces safety even if:

- policies are misconfigured
- predicates are missing
- workflows are incomplete

NAS is the final safety boundary.

---

The BaseLayerOS Governance Model provides:

- constitutional enforcement
- identity‑bound execution
- regulatory alignment
- workflow compliance
- governed data access
- deterministic runtime behavior
- forensic traceability
- explainability
- safe evolution
- hard safety boundaries

This governance model is what makes BaseLayerOS a **true governance OS** suitable for:

- finance
- healthcare
- government
- critical infrastructure
- enterprise automation
- cross‑industry platforms
